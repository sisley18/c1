Transcript: "Persuasion, Ethics, and the Human Mind — A Cross-Disciplinary Perspective"

Welcome to Thinking Forward, the podcast that brings together ideas from across the academic spectrum. Today we have Dr. Helena Marsh, a professor of applied philosophy and cognitive science at the University of Edinburgh, here to discuss the intersection of rhetoric, bioethics, international affairs, and human cognition.

Dr. Marsh, let's start with rhetoric. How relevant is it in the twenty-first century?

Enormously relevant. Aristotle identified three modes of persuasion — ethos, pathos, and logos — over two thousand years ago, and they remain the foundation of every persuasive communication today. What has changed is the medium. In the digital age, algorithms create filter bubbles that reinforce existing beliefs rather than challenging them, and the rise of deepfakes has blurred the line between authentic and manufactured discourse. The result is what many scholars call a crisis of rhetoric.

That connects to bioethics too, doesn't it?

Absolutely. Take CRISPR gene-editing technology. It has the potential to eradicate hereditary diseases like sickle cell anaemia, but it also opens the door to designer babies — where parents select traits like intelligence or physical appearance. Critics warn this could create a new form of inequality based on genetic privilege. The ethical questions around germline editing — modifications passed down to future generations — demand the kind of rigorous argumentation that rhetoric teaches us.

And how does geopolitics fit into this picture?

Well, the world has shifted from a unipolar order after the Soviet Union collapsed in nineteen ninety-one to a complex multipolar system. China, India, and Brazil have emerged as major powers. Joseph Nye's concept of soft power — influencing others through culture and values rather than military force — has become central to diplomacy. South Korea's Korean Wave is a perfect example: K-pop and dramas have generated billions in revenue and enormous diplomatic goodwill.

Finally, what role does cognitive science play in all of this?

A crucial one. Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky showed that humans are far less rational than we assumed. Cognitive biases like confirmation bias — seeking information that supports what we already believe — and the anchoring effect — being swayed by the first information we receive — distort our judgment in politics, medicine, and everyday decisions. Metacognition, the ability to think about our own thinking, is the antidote. Research shows that students who practise metacognitive strategies consistently outperform those who don't. The prefrontal cortex is the brain region most closely linked to these reflective processes.
